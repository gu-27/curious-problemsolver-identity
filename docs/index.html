Conceptual & Research Foundation

The Curious Problem Solver

A Process-Based Identity Framework for Sustainable Growth in Changing Environments

John Correia · Founder, Inland NW AI · Professor of Information Systems, Gonzaga University

This framework emerged from years of teaching and industry engagement at the intersection of technology, organizational change, and human performance. It synthesizes research across identity psychology, mindfulness neuroscience, first-principles problem solving, and habit formation to propose a unified approach to culture transformation and individual growth in rapidly changing environments.

Section 01
The Foundation

Why Identity, Not Skills

Most organizations invest in skill-building: master this tool, achieve this metric, complete this certification. But research in behavior change consistently shows that sustainable transformation begins not with skills, but with identity.

From James Clear's work synthesizing identity-based habit formation: your current behaviors reflect your current identity. When you shift from "I need to learn AI" to "I am a curious problem solver," your behaviors follow naturally and durably. This is not motivational language. It is supported by neuroscience and behavioral psychology. Repeated identity-aligned actions rewire the brain for lasting change.

Michelle Carter's research on IT Identity provides the empirical foundation specific to technology adoption: IT identity — defined as the extent to which an individual views use of technology as integral to their sense of self — predicts both exploratory and feature-based technology usage in ways that existing competency or skill models do not. Identity precedes behavior. Identity sustains behavior where training alone cannot.

Process-Based vs. Outcome-Based Identity

A process-based identity is habitual and automatic. When you face uncertainty, your identity is your first reaction — not your deliberate choice. "I am a curious problem solver" means curiosity and problem definition are your default response to ambiguity, not techniques you remember to apply.

An outcome-based identity ("I am an AI expert," "I am successful") is fragile. When the outcome shifts, and in today's AI landscape it will, the identity fractures. A process-based identity ("I am someone who asks questions and solves problems rigorously") is resilient. It survives failure, adaptation, and transformation because the process, not the outcome, is who you are.

This distinction matters significantly for organizations navigating AI adoption. Employees and leaders who anchor their identities to mastery of specific tools resist change when those tools evolve. Those who anchor to the process of curious, rigorous problem-solving adapt, lead others through uncertainty, and create sustained value.

Section 02
The Structure

The Two Dimensions — A Yin-Yang Structure

The Curious Problem Solver identity has two essential dimensions. They are not sequential. They are not hierarchical. Each is incomplete without the other — curiosity without rigor becomes waste; rigor without curiosity becomes rigidity.

Dimension 1

Curiosity

An objective stance toward new things — staying open and exploratory rather than reactive or judgmental.

Dimension 2

Problem Solving

Curiosity directed at problems worth solving. If you solve problems, you add value.

Dimension 1 — Curiosity: The Foundation of Objectivity

Curiosity, in this framework, is grounded in contemporary mindfulness neuroscience and Eastern contemplative traditions applied to modern psychology.

Judson Brewer's research on habit change reveals that curiosity is fundamentally non-judgmental. When you approach a setback, a new tool, or an unfamiliar situation with judgment — "I shouldn't feel this way," "This threatens my expertise" — you activate reactivity and avoidance. Curiosity interrupts that cycle. It asks: "What is actually happening here? What can I observe? What am I not yet seeing?" This shift from evaluation to observation is the core mechanism.

This connects directly to Eastern mindfulness traditions and the concept of beginner's mind — approaching each situation as if for the first time, without the accumulated weight of prior conclusions. In organizational contexts, curiosity is what allows an executive to say "I don't fully understand this yet" without shame, and a team to ask "What went wrong?" rather than "Who failed?"

Research also shows that curiosity correlates strongly with growth mindset, openness to feedback, reduced confirmation bias, and higher overall well-being, making it not just a professional asset but a human one.

Dimension 2 — Problem Solving: Grounding Curiosity in Rigor

Curiosity without a problem to solve generates interesting observations that never land. Problem solving is what makes curiosity valuable. It gives exploration a destination and ensures that what you learn creates value for someone. This is a documented failure pattern in organizational AI investment: teams explore fascinating capabilities that solve no defined problem, generating expense and disillusionment rather than value.

Problem solving in this framework uses first-principles thinking as a lens — not a rigid procedure, but a habit of mind that asks: what is actually true here, stripped of assumption?

The Foundations of First Principles Thinking

First principles thinking has roots that span more than two millennia. Aristotle defined a first principle as "the first basis from which a thing is known" — a foundational truth that cannot be deduced from anything else. In his Metaphysics and Physics, Aristotle argued that genuine understanding requires tracing any phenomenon back to its irreducible elements: not what we assume to be true, not what convention tells us, but what we can verify as foundational.

René Descartes formalized this approach in the 17th century through what is now called Cartesian Doubt: systematically questioning every assumption until only indubitable truths remain, then rebuilding knowledge from that solid foundation. This method became a cornerstone of the modern scientific tradition and the basis of empirical inquiry.

In contemporary practice, first-principles thinking has been applied across engineering, strategy consulting, product design, and innovation. Elon Musk's approach to SpaceX — refusing to accept the received wisdom that rockets were inherently expensive, decomposing the problem to raw materials and physics, and rebuilding from there — is a widely cited modern example. The insight: most "impossible" constraints are inherited assumptions, not foundational truths. When you strip them away, the solution space opens dramatically.

Applied to problem solving in organizations, first principles ask: What is the actual problem — not the symptom, not the assumed solution, but the problem itself? Can you state it simply enough to evaluate whether you've solved it? This discipline forces precision and prevents the common failure mode of building solutions to problems that were never clearly defined.

When curiosity meets first-principles rigor, several things follow:

  • Exploration becomes grounded — you follow curiosity into AI capabilities, but always return to: does this solve a real, defined problem?
  • Waste decreases — not every interesting capability gets built; only those aligned to verified needs
  • Value becomes communicable — you can explain not just what you built, but why it matters and how you know it worked
  • Learning compounds — each problem solved teaches you something transferable about how to solve the next one
Section 03
The Identity in Practice

The Integration — What It Means to Be a Curious Problem Solver

When this identity is internalized, these orientations become automatic. They don't follow a strict sequence — the order shifts with the situation — but each surfaces naturally when facing uncertainty or ambiguity:

1
Curiosity First
"What is actually happening? What am I missing? What biases am I bringing to this?"
2
Problem Definition
"What is the core problem? How would I know if it were solved?"
3
Disciplined Exploration
"What solutions are grounded in this problem? What does the evidence suggest?"
4
Iteration as Normal
"Did this work? What did we learn? What do we do differently?"
This identity is not situational. It does not switch off under pressure. It gets stronger through use, both through success, which confirms the identity, and through challenge, which requires defending and deepening it.

A Framework for Critical Thinkers

The Curious Problem Solver identity is, at its core, a critical thinking identity. Critical thinking requires the same two dimensions: openness to what is actually true (curiosity) and precision about what the actual question is (problem definition). Most critical thinking models describe skills to apply. This framework describes an identity to inhabit.

When critical thinking becomes identity, the sequence changes. The curious problem solver asks "What do I actually know here?" before "What should I do?" That ordering distinguishes critical from reactive thinking. It is not a technique retrieved under pressure — it is the default.

Universality and Well-Being

The framework's power extends beyond technology. A curious problem solver approaches a team conflict, a business pivot, a personal setback, or a new market with the same identity: curiosity about what is actually true, precision about what the actual problem is, and disciplined iteration toward a grounded solution.

This universality is intentional and practical. Research on curiosity, growth mindset, and process-based identity consistently connects these orientations to lower anxiety, higher engagement, greater resilience, and sustained well-being. Building a curious problem solver identity is not just an organizational change strategy. It is an investment in sustained performance and well-being.

Section 05
Organizational Application

Implications for Organizations

Organizations struggling with AI adoption and culture change are often fighting identity battles they have not named. Employees who have anchored their identities to mastery of specific systems feel threatened by AI's uncertainty. Teams rewarded for executing fixed plans struggle with the ambiguity of exploration. Neither training nor mandates resolve this — identity does.

The Curious Problem Solver framework offers a path that is neither "embrace all change" nor "resist all change." It is: develop the identity that helps you stay curious about what change means for your actual problems, then solve those problems with rigor.

Leaders who model this identity — who openly acknowledge what they don't know, who ask first-principles questions before proposing solutions, who treat iteration as normal rather than failure — create psychological safety for others to do the same. Culture shifts when identity shifts.

The unit of culture transformation is not the policy or the training program. It is the identity of the people inside the organization — and identity is changed by practice, not by mandate.
